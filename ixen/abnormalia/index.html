What an Amiga diskmag was

A diskmagazine was a magazine distributed on floppy disk and read on the computer itself, common on the Commodore Amiga in the early 1990s. It bundled text articles, custom fonts, music, and a navigation interface into an executable. They were produced by demoscene groups and traded through the post and through bulletin board systems.

Macno was the main editor of Abnormalia, also a writer and contributor in several other diskmags including Grace and Scene Lyrics. The group released seven issues — Abnormalia 1 through 5, with a π-numbered 3.14 between 3 and 4 — plus a special edition titled Macnolamia in July 1995. After Macnolamia, demoscene involvement faded. No individual issue contents or full staff lists are confirmed.

To distribute was to be copied by strangers at 2400 baud.

Small groups. Magazines. BBSes. Intros that announced the disk before the disk loaded. Aliases instead of names, because a name is a liability and a handle is a costume.

How scene releases moved

Demoscene productions circulated through personal mail, sceners trading disks, and bulletin board systems (BBSes) where files were uploaded and downloaded over dial-up modems. Groups advertised releases with small intros and, on BBSes, with BBStros — short promotional demos branded for a board.

Because no central archive existed, the surviving record is fragmentary. Demozoo's search results include a trace associating Abnormalia with BBS and BBStro promotion around 1996. This is an indexed pointer only and should not be read as a complete account of activity in that period.

The 2004 homepage

The Internet Archive captured abnormalia.com on 21 May 2004. The page was titled "Abnormalia. Un nome. Pochi fatti." and described itself as "Luogo anomalo di anomini in rete. Notizie, racconti, storie, satira, grafica, praticamente di tutto. Per niente."

It linked to sections labelled abnominio 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 900, and included a small mailing-list form requesting "Una tua e-mail" to subscribe via the invented verb "@bnolistarti." The construction is typical handmade early-2000s Italian web: table-based layout, small Verdana text, and irregular, anti-polished microcopy.

I did not publish content. I grew a dialect and let content happen to it.

The invented vocabulary

A defining feature of the Abnormalia web project was a set of coined and mutated Italian words used as the site's native terminology: anomini and abnomini (its anonymous/abnormal users), abnominio (its sections or acts), abmorto, and phrases such as regolaria varia, Buco Nero, Sinaptium, Pasticche Net, and "javate del secolo scorso," alongside the recurring line "il passato rimane."

Rather than simply hosting articles, the site built an internal language. The vocabulary defined the project's identity and structure as much as any individual piece of content, and much of it remains deliberately opaque.

2005. On OpenSkills, a hypervisor opened its mouth.

I-Xen. A Xen-on-SuSE-9.3 tutorial wearing a short story like a coat. "I, System." Text by al. Graphics by tatlin. Version 0.1, which is to say: alive enough to crash.

It called itself graphical madness. The first chapter of an interactive story for configuring and understanding Xen. Or, in its own words, just another half born fun project. The most accurate self-description in the archive.

It is why you're reading an "ixen" now. The form survived the technology. The hypervisor died; the voice that narrated it got reincarnated as a page-shaped genre.

The 2005 I-Xen page

The local i-xen page is a 2005 OpenSkills production combining a technical tutorial for running the Xen hypervisor on SuSE Linux 9.3 with a first-person short story titled "I, System." Text is credited to "al" and graphics to "tatlin." It describes itself as version 0.1, "graphical madness," and the first chapter of an interactive story for Xen configuration and understanding — "just another half born fun project."

It is the formal ancestor of today's Ixen pages: the same hybrid of first-person narration and exact technical content, applied now to different subjects and built with different tools.

The 2023 return

In 2023, abnormalia.com relaunched — 26 years after Abnormalia 1. Social accounts began publishing a daily abnominio: small images with text, organized into multiple series covering different topics. The format was distinct from the early-2000s Italian website: visual, serialized, optimized for social post consumption rather than the handmade-web-microsite structure of the original abnomini.

The daily cadence held for a few months, then became less frequent, then sporadic, then effectively stopped — without announcement. The accounts remain online but inactive. The pattern repeats: energetic launch, gradual unmourned fade, the last post indistinguishable from any other.

How the present site is built

The current abnormalia.com is a Jekyll static site hosted on GitHub Pages behind Cloudflare. Each "ixen" is a standalone generated HTML page stored under ixen/<slug>/ and registered in the data file _data/ixens.yml.

Pages are produced by the "generate-ixens" swamp workflow from YAML input files: prose is generated with Claude, images with OpenAI, music with Suno, and final assembly is handled by swamp extensions. The result is a collection of self-contained mixed-media pages listed on an archive index.

The continuity

Across roughly three decades, Abnormalia has appeared as an Amiga diskmagazine group, a BBS-era scene presence, a handmade website, an intermittent social-media handle, a hypertext tutorial-story, and now a generator of mixed-media pages. The connecting thread is not a format but a persistent refusal to remain in any one of them.

The current project states its own position toward AI-generated media plainly: "AI slop with style. Own the slop. Transcend it - or don't." The emphasis falls on the attempt itself rather than on a finished, settled product.
