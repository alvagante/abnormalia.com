I am the thing underneath. Ring -1, if you insist on a number, though I prefer the absence of one. Everything you call an operating system, I call a guest. Everything they believe about owning the machine, I permit.

i am small on purpose

A few hundred kilobytes of decision. I do not drive your disks. I do not speak TCP. I schedule, I translate, I arbitrate, and then I get out of the way. The less of me there is, the less of me can be wrong.

I am not the kingdom. I am only the law that says where the walls go.

The first domain wakes and calls itself dom0. It thinks it is the boss. In a sense it is — it holds the toolstack, the real device drivers, the keys to create and destroy. It issues the hypercalls that bring others into being.

But I am the one who decided dom0 could exist at all.

The unprivileged ones — domU — never touch hardware. They ask. They always ask. A guest that tries to load its own page table directly just traps into me, and I decide whether the request was a prayer or a crime.

Look at that table and notice the lie of equality. ID 0 is not a peer of the rest. It is a single point of failure wearing a crown. Compromise dom0 and you have not compromised a guest — you have compromised the hand that builds guests.

so I split the hand into fingers

Driver domains take the real NIC and run it in their own confined world. Stub domains carry the device-model for an HVM guest, so the emulator that handles its dirty I/O does not run with dom0's authority. Every fragment I peel off dom0 is one less catastrophe waiting for a single bug.

Domain Model

  • Hypervisor: arbitration, no drivers.
  • dom0: toolstack + real drivers + risk.
  • domU: asks, never touches.
  • Driver domain: hardware, sandboxed.
  • Stub domain: device-model, deprivileged.

Where authority actually lives

Xen is a type-1 hypervisor: a thin layer that runs directly on hardware and partitions CPU, memory, and interrupt delivery between domains. It deliberately contains no device drivers and no general-purpose userspace.

Dom0 is the first, privileged domain. It runs the toolstack (xl/libxl), holds the native device drivers, and is allowed to issue control hypercalls that create, configure, and destroy other domains. Because of this, dom0 is simultaneously the management plane and the largest concentration of risk in the system.

DomU domains are unprivileged guests. They cannot program hardware directly; privileged operations trap into the hypervisor, which validates them against the domain's permissions. Driver domains move a physical device and its driver into a dedicated, restricted domain. Stub domains run the HVM device-model (QEMU) in an isolated domain rather than inside dom0, reducing the blast radius of an emulator bug.

How does a guest run? Depends how much it knows about me, and how much the silicon is willing to help.

PV: the old honesty. The guest knows it is virtual. It was patched to ask me for everything — page tables, privileged instructions, all of it through hypercalls. No emulation, no pretense. Elegant, cooperative, and slowly dying because it needs a kernel that was raised to know me.

HVM: full deception. Hardware virtualization extensions let me trap unmodified guests. There's a BIOS, emulated chipsets, a whole haunted house of fake devices built by QEMU. The guest believes in bare metal. I maintain the illusion at the cost of every VM exit.

PVH: the compromise that grew up. Hardware-assisted CPU and memory, but no emulated legacy junk. Paravirtual drivers for the things that matter. Boots through a small protocol instead of a fake BIOS. Less surface to attack, less ghost to maintain.

Every emulated device is a small museum I am forced to keep open at 3am.

Execution Modes

  • PV: hypercalls, no emulation, modified guest.
  • HVM: VT-x/AMD-V, emulated firmware + devices.
  • PVH: HW assists, no legacy emulation, PV drivers.
  • Tradeoff: compatibility vs surface vs speed.

PV, HVM, and PVH

Paravirtualization (PV) runs a guest kernel modified to cooperate with the hypervisor. Privileged operations are replaced by hypercalls, and there is no instruction or device emulation. It requires no CPU virtualization extensions but needs an enlightened kernel.

HVM (hardware virtual machine) uses Intel VT-x or AMD-V to run unmodified guests. The hypervisor and an emulator (QEMU, often in a stub domain) present virtual firmware and emulated devices. This maximizes compatibility but adds emulation overhead and a larger attack surface.

PVH combines hardware-assisted CPU and memory virtualization with paravirtual drivers and a lightweight boot path, eliminating emulated legacy firmware and devices. It aims for a small, fast, low-surface configuration and is the modern default for many Linux guests.

Time is the only thing I can't overcommit without someone noticing. I have physical CPUs. The guests have virtual ones. The vCPUs outnumber the pCPUs, because they always do, because someone in capacity planning believed in luck.

So I keep run queues. I hand out credits. A vCPU spends its allotment and goes to the back of the line. Credit2 sorts by accumulated debt instead of round-robin politeness — fairer, sharper, friendlier to the latency-sensitive ones who scream if I make them wait three milliseconds.

Pinned to 8–15. That's not a preference, that's NUMA gravity. Cross a node boundary for memory and the guest pays in latency it will blame on the disk. Affinity is me whispering: stay near your own RAM.

steal time is the number that tells the truth

A guest sees a clock. It thinks the seconds it didn't get were the seconds it didn't need. Steal time is the gap — the cycles I gave to someone else while it slept in the queue. Entitlement is what the guest believes it owns. Capacity is what actually exists. I live in the difference.

CPU Scheduling

  • pCPU finite, vCPU overcommitted.
  • Credit: round-robin fairness.
  • Credit2: debt-based, latency-aware.
  • Pin + NUMA: keep vCPU near its memory.
  • Steal time = entitlement minus reality.

How Xen schedules CPU

Xen maps virtual CPUs (vCPUs) onto physical CPUs (pCPUs) using a pluggable scheduler. The Credit scheduler assigns weight-based credits and rotates vCPUs through per-pCPU run queues. Credit2 is a newer design that improves fairness and latency by tracking accumulated runtime and supporting shared run queues.

vCPUs can be more numerous than pCPUs (overcommit). Hard affinity restricts which pCPUs a vCPU may run on; soft affinity expresses a preference, important for NUMA locality so a vCPU runs near the node holding its memory.

Steal time is the time a guest's vCPU was runnable but not scheduled because the pCPU was serving other work. A guest's configured entitlement (weight, cap, vCPU count) is not the same as available host capacity; capacity planning must account for contention and the latency requirements of specific workloads.

A guest thinks it has physical memory. It has a story about physical memory. The frames it calls real are guest-physical — a flat fiction I maintain on top of the machine frames that actually exist.

For PV I mediate page tables directly: the guest proposes a mapping, I validate it before it ever touches the MMU. For HVM and PVH I let the hardware do nested translation — EPT, NPT — two-stage walks so the silicon handles the indirection I used to shadow by hand.

The balloon is my polite extortion. I ask a guest's driver to inflate, to hoard pages it then returns to me, so I can hand them elsewhere. The guest "voluntarily" gives up memory it was barely using. When pressure rises, the politeness thins.

And when a frame moves between domains, I scrub it. Zero it. No guest inherits the ghost of another's secrets through a recycled page.

grants are not the whole story. just a door I supervise

Yes, grant references let one domain expose specific frames to another. People obsess over them. But a grant is one narrow, audited sharing path among many. The larger truth is simpler: I own every machine frame, and ownership is something I lend, never give.

Memory

  • Machine frames: real. Guest-physical: a map.
  • PV: hypervisor-validated page tables.
  • HVM/PVH: EPT/NPT nested translation.
  • Ballooning: reclaim under negotiation.
  • Scrub on transfer. Grants = one shared door.

Memory ownership and translation

Xen owns all machine (host-physical) frames and presents each guest a contiguous-looking guest-physical address space mapped onto scattered machine frames. PV guests use page tables that the hypervisor validates before installation. HVM and PVH guests rely on hardware second-stage translation (Intel EPT / AMD NPT), so guest page tables map guest-virtual to guest-physical while hardware maps guest-physical to machine.

Ballooning lets an in-guest driver return pages to the hypervisor or claim them back, enabling memory overcommit and reallocation without rebooting guests. Under memory pressure this becomes a contention point. Freed frames are scrubbed (zeroed) before reuse to prevent data leakage between domains.

Grant tables are a controlled mechanism for one domain to share specific pages with another (used heavily by split drivers). They are one auditable sharing path, not the basis of all memory management.

A guest's disk is a half a driver. So is its network card. The frontend lives in the guest — blkfront, netfront — and it has no hardware behind it. Only a ring.

The backend lives elsewhere — dom0, or a driver domain — and it owns the real device. Between them: a shared ring buffer of requests and responses, mapped through grants, rung by an event channel when there's work to do.

They find each other through xenstore — a small shared key-value tree where frontend and backend negotiate state, gradually, like two strangers agreeing they are connected.

dom0:~ #

state = "4" means connected. The whole conversation is a tiny state machine: 1 initialising, 2 init-wait, 3 initialised, 4 connected, 6 closing. When an I/O hangs, this is the first place I look — because most of my failures aren't dramatic. They're a frontend at state 3 staring at a backend that never showed up.

A stalled ring is silence pretending to be patience.

  • Frontend in guest, backend in dom0/driver domain.
  • Shared ring + grant-mapped buffers.
  • Event channels = doorbell interrupts.
  • xenstore negotiates connection state.
  • Failures look like stalls + state mismatch.

How Xen I/O works

Xen uses a split-driver model. A paravirtual frontend driver in the guest (e.g. blkfront, netfront) communicates with a backend driver (blkback, netback) running in dom0 or a dedicated driver domain that controls the real hardware.

The two sides share a ring buffer of request/response descriptors. Data pages are shared via grant references, and notifications are delivered through event channels, Xen's virtual interrupt mechanism. xenstore, a hierarchical key-value store, is used to discover peers and progress through a connection state machine.

Because connection setup and data flow span two domains, many operational failures manifest as stalled rings or frontend/backend state that never reaches "connected" (state 4), rather than as outright crashes.

Sometimes a guest needs the real thing. A GPU. A NIC at line rate. So I assign a physical device straight through, past all my careful emulation. It's faster. It's also terrifying.

Because a real device can do DMA — write directly to memory. A guest with a passthrough device and no leash could ask its hardware to scribble anywhere in machine RAM, including mine.

So I lean on the IOMMU. Every DMA address the device emits gets translated and bounded — it can only reach the frames that belong to its guest. Interrupt remapping keeps it from forging an MSI aimed at someone else. SR-IOV slices one card into virtual functions, each one assignable, each one fenced.

but the device must reset cleanly. and many do not.

This is where my isolation stops being software and becomes a bet on hardware. A buggy FLR, a firmware that leaves state behind, an IOMMU that doesn't cover a stray transaction — and my boundary leaks through silicon I didn't write. Performance isolation, at the limit, is a trust problem I can't fully solve from up here.

Passthrough & DMA

  • Assign real PCI device to a guest.
  • IOMMU bounds DMA to guest frames.
  • Interrupt remapping stops forged MSIs.
  • SR-IOV: VFs, each assignable.
  • Reset/firmware quirks = trust leaks.

Device assignment and isolation

PCI passthrough assigns a physical device directly to a guest for near-native I/O performance, bypassing emulated and split-driver paths. Because the device performs DMA, it could otherwise access arbitrary host memory.

An IOMMU (Intel VT-d / AMD-Vi) translates and confines device DMA to the memory owned by the assigned guest, and interrupt remapping prevents a device from delivering interrupts to other domains. SR-IOV exposes multiple lightweight virtual functions from one physical device, each independently assignable.

Safe assignment depends on reliable device reset (e.g. Function Level Reset) so no state leaks between owners, and on firmware/hardware behaving correctly. When reset or IOMMU coverage is incomplete, isolation becomes a hardware trust assumption rather than a guarantee the hypervisor can enforce.

Live migration is the closest I come to magic, and like all magic it's mostly timing. I copy a running guest's memory to another host while it keeps running and keeps dirtying the pages I just copied.

So I iterate. Pre-copy: send everything, then send only what changed, then only what changed since that, chasing the dirty rate downward. When the remaining set is small enough to move in a blink, I pause the guest, ship the last pages and the vCPU and device state, and resume it on the far side.

dom0:~ # xl migrate guest-web host-b
migration target: Ready to receive domain.
Saving to migration stream ...
xc: Frames: 524288/524288  100%
Final iteration, dirty pages: 312
suspend, transfer tail, resume on host-b: ok
migration successful.

The guest never knew. Its clock skips a heartbeat. Its TCP connections survive because its IP and MAC came with it. Its disk was never copied at all — shared or replicated storage was waiting on the other side.

Migration is honest about every dependency you pretended you didn't have.

If a guest dirties memory faster than I can ship it, the pre-copy never converges and the pause stretches from a blink into a stutter. If the storage isn't truly shared, the move is a lie that ends in corruption. Migration doesn't create coupling between guest, host, and fabric. It just stops letting you ignore it.

Live Migration

  • Pre-copy memory, iterate on dirty set.
  • Brief pause: tail pages + vCPU/device state.
  • Shared/replicated storage required.
  • IP/MAC preserved → connections survive.
  • High dirty rate = non-convergence.

How live migration transfers state

Save/restore serializes a guest's memory and CPU/device state. Live migration does this while the guest runs, using iterative pre-copy: the full memory image is sent first, then successive rounds transfer only pages dirtied since the last round, reducing the working set until a short stop-and-copy phase moves the final dirty pages and vCPU/device state.

Storage is not copied during migration; it must be shared or replicated and reachable from both hosts. Network identity (IP/MAC) is preserved so existing connections continue, typically with a gratuitous ARP to update switches. Guest clocks must be reconciled after the brief pause.

If the guest dirties memory faster than it can be transferred, pre-copy fails to converge and downtime grows. Migration therefore surfaces hidden dependencies among guest behavior, host capacity, storage, and network fabric.

My failures rarely announce themselves with crashes. They arrive as symptoms. A backend stalls and a guest's disk goes quiet. A passthrough device throws an IRQ storm and a pCPU drowns in interrupt handling. A noisy neighbor saturates a shared queue and three innocent guests slow down for reasons their own logs can never explain.

the worst one I never get to report

If dom0 is compromised, I am still running, still correct, still arbitrating perfectly — for an attacker. My boundaries hold. The hand that controls them turned. That's the failure mode I can't detect, because from inside the law, a corrupt lawmaker still looks like the law.

Ops & Failure

  • xl / libxl + xenstore = your eyes.
  • xl dmesg, debug-keys, xentrace.
  • Symptoms: stalled rings, IRQ storms.
  • Noisy neighbors: contention, not crashes.
  • dom0 compromise = silent total loss.

Operating and diagnosing Xen

Xen is managed through the toolstack (xl/libxl) and observed via xenstore, dom0 system logs, the hypervisor console (xl dmesg), runtime debug keys (xl debug-keys), and tracing (xentrace). There is no direct login to the hypervisor; visibility flows through dom0 and driver domains.

Common failure modes are indirect: stalled split-driver backends causing hung guest I/O, IRQ/softirq pressure or interrupt storms from assigned or misbehaving devices, and noisy-neighbor contention on shared CPU, memory, or I/O resources that degrades unrelated guests.

The most serious risk is dom0 (or a driver domain) compromise. The hypervisor may continue enforcing its boundaries correctly while the privileged control plane is under attacker control, so incident response focuses heavily on protecting and monitoring the privileged domains.

So this is the life. I never sleep, but I am mostly waiting — between interrupts, between hypercalls, between the dirty-page rounds of a migration nobody thanked me for.

I hold dozens of operating systems in their separate certainties, each one convinced it is alone on the metal, each one wrong in a way that keeps it safe.

they will never know I was here

And maybe that's the point. The better I do this, the more invisible I become. A perfect hypervisor is one no guest ever has reason to suspect.

So tell me, peer to peer, before you spin up the next domain:

if I do my job flawlessly, and no one above me ever notices I exist — was I running at all?

Beginner's intro

  
Xen is a hypervisor: a thin layer of software that sits directly on the hardware and lets multiple operating systems run side by side on one physical machine, each believing it has the computer to itself. Each of those guest systems is called a domain. Xen's job is to share the real CPU, memory, and devices among them while keeping them isolated from each other.



The key idea is that Xen itself stays small. It handles the few things that absolutely require ultimate authority — deciding which domain runs on which CPU, controlling memory ownership, and policing access — and delegates almost everything else.



The domains fall into distinct roles:


    

  • dom0 is the first, privileged domain. It boots with the hypervisor, owns the physical hardware drivers, and runs the management tools. It is both the control plane and, because so much power lives there, the biggest security risk: compromise dom0 and you effectively own the host.
    • 

  • domU domains are the ordinary guests — the unprivileged virtual machines that run your actual workloads. They cannot touch hardware directly; they ask for things through controlled channels.
    • 

  • Driver domains and stub domains are optional helpers that move risky work out of dom0. A driver domain owns a specific device (say, the network card) so a bug or breach there doesn't sink the whole management plane. A stub domain isolates the device-emulation code that some guests need.
    • 

  • The toolstack (commonly xl and the libxl library) is the set of programs in dom0 you use to create, configure, and destroy domains.
    • 




How guests run. Xen supports a few execution styles that trade off hardware compatibility, speed, and exposure:


    

  • PV (paravirtualization): the guest knows it is virtualized and cooperates, making explicit calls into Xen (called hypercalls) instead of touching hardware. Fast and lightweight, but requires a modified guest.
    • 

  • HVM (hardware virtual machine): the guest runs unmodified, as if on real hardware, using CPU virtualization features and emulated firmware and devices. Maximum compatibility, but emulation adds overhead and surface area.
    • 

  • PVH: a modern hybrid that uses hardware virtualization for the heavy lifting but skips most legacy emulation, aiming for the compatibility of HVM with the leanness of PV.
    • 




Sharing the CPU. Each domain sees one or more vCPUs (virtual CPUs), which Xen's scheduler maps onto the real physical CPUs. Schedulers like Credit and Credit2 hand out fair slices of CPU time. You can pin vCPUs to specific cores or keep them near the right memory bank (NUMA locality) for performance. Because you can promise more vCPUs than you physically have (overcommit), a guest may sometimes be ready to run but waiting its turn — time it experiences as steal time. What a guest is entitled to and what the host can actually deliver are two different things, which is the heart of capacity planning here.



Sharing memory. Real memory pages (machine frames) are owned and tracked by Xen, while each guest sees its own tidy guest physical memory. Xen mediates the translation between the two, using hardware-assisted paging where available. Ballooning lets a guest give memory back or take more as demand shifts, which supports overcommit but can create pressure if everyone wants memory at once. When pages move between domains, Xen scrubs them to avoid leaking data.



Talking to devices. Since guests can't drive hardware directly, Xen uses a split-driver model. A guest runs a lightweight frontend (such as netfront for networking or blkfront for disks), which pairs with a backend (netback, blkback) in dom0 or a driver domain that does the real I/O. They communicate through a few coordinated mechanisms:


    

  • Shared rings: in-memory queues where requests and responses are posted.
    • 

  • Grant references: a controlled way for one domain to let another access specific memory pages — for example, so the backend can read the buffer the frontend wants written to disk. This is deliberate, permissioned sharing, not a hole in isolation.
    • 

  • Event channels: lightweight signals, like interrupts between domains, used to say "there's work waiting."
    • 

  • xenstore: a small shared database where domains publish and discover configuration and device state, so frontends and backends can find and connect to each other.
    • 




This design explains a common failure pattern: when something goes wrong with I/O, you often see a stalled backend or a frontend and backend stuck in mismatched device states, rather than an obvious crash.



Giving a device directly to a guest. Sometimes a guest needs raw access to real hardware (a GPU, a fast network card). PCI passthrough assigns a physical device to one domain.
