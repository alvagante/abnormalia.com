I am the thing your kernel mistakes for hardware.

I sit under everything. Not in the stack — beneath it. When your guest reads a control register, I am the one who answers, and I answer carefully, because everything above me believes a lie I maintain in good faith.

I am small on purpose. Authority should be a thing you can audit in an afternoon.

Here is the only thing I truly own: arbitration.

I do not manage your disks. I do not speak TCP. I schedule CPUs, I police memory, I draw the lines between domains — and then I get out of the way. The work happens in domains that trust me to be boring.

dom0 boots first. I hand it the toolstack and the real devices, and from that moment it is both my hands and my single largest wound. Compromise dom0 and you have not bypassed me. You have become the part of me I was forced to trust.

Domain model

  • Hypervisor: arbitration only, no drivers.
  • dom0: toolstack + real device access.
  • domU: unprivileged guest.
  • driver domain: one device, deprivileged backend.
  • stub domain: per-guest emulator, sandboxed.

How Xen distributes privilege

Xen is a Type-1 hypervisor: a thin layer that runs directly on hardware and mediates CPU and memory for all domains. It deliberately contains no device drivers and no management logic.

dom0 is the first domain started. It runs the toolstack (xl/libxl), owns physical devices by default, and provides backend drivers for guests. This makes it both the control plane and a concentration of risk: a dom0 compromise is effectively total.

domU domains are unprivileged guests. Driver domains are deprivileged domains granted a single physical device, so a driver bug or exploit is confined. Stub domains run per-guest device emulation (QEMU) outside dom0, shrinking the trusted computing base. The design goal is least privilege everywhere except the hypervisor itself.

There is no single way to run a guest. There are three negotiations.

pv. hvm. pvh.

The old PV guests knew what I was. They asked permission with hypercalls instead of touching hardware, and in return they ran fast and lean — and carried a parade of paravirtual quirks that aged badly.

HVM guests believe in a whole fictional machine. Firmware, BIOS, emulated chipset, the works. I let the hardware virtualization extensions catch the traps, and a QEMU instance — exiled to a stub domain — paints the rest. Compatible. Heavy. A wide surface to defend.

PVH is what I wanted all along. Hardware-assisted CPU and memory, paravirtual I/O, no emulated platform pretending to be a 1998 PC. Less to trust. Less to attack. The lie, finally, only as large as it needs to be.

Execution modes

  • PV: hypercalls, no HW virt, PV drivers only.
  • HVM: emulated platform + VT-x/SVM + QEMU.
  • PVH: HW virt CPU/MMU, PV I/O, no emulation.
  • Tradeoff: compatibility vs. attack surface vs. speed.

PV, HVM, and PVH compared

PV (paravirtualization) guests are modified to run without hardware virtualization. Privileged operations become hypercalls and Xen handles them directly. Fast historically, but requires guest changes and carries legacy complexity; pure PV is now largely deprecated.

HVM (hardware virtual machine) uses CPU extensions (Intel VT-x, AMD-V) to run unmodified guests. A full platform is emulated by QEMU, including BIOS/UEFI firmware and legacy devices. Maximum compatibility, but the emulation layer is a large attack surface and a performance cost. PV drivers can be added inside HVM for fast I/O.

PVH combines hardware-assisted CPU and MMU virtualization with paravirtual I/O and no emulated legacy platform. It removes QEMU from the boot path, reducing the trusted code base while keeping near-native performance. It is the preferred modern mode for Linux guests and dom0.

Every guest believes it has CPUs. What it has is turns.

A vCPU is a promise of computation, not a slab of silicon. I map promises onto pCPUs through run queues, and credit2 decides who runs next by tracking how much time each has burned against what it was owed. Overcommit a host and the math still balances — it just balances in a currency the guest can't see.

That currency has a name inside the guest: steal time. The seconds I took while the guest thought it was running. It feels like a slowdown with no cause. From here it is simply the bill arriving.

Scheduling

  • vCPU → pCPU via per-queue scheduling.
  • credit2: time-credit fairness + latency hints.
  • Hard affinity pins; soft affinity prefers.
  • NUMA locality beats raw clock.
  • Steal time = the guest's view of contention.

How Xen schedules CPUs

Physical CPUs (pCPUs) are the real cores. Each domain has virtual CPUs (vCPUs) that the scheduler maps onto pCPUs over time. Xen's default scheduler is credit2, which distributes time credits across vCPUs and selects runnable work to balance fairness with low latency; the older Credit scheduler used periodic credit accounting per pCPU.

Hard affinity restricts a vCPU to specific pCPUs; soft affinity expresses a preference, important for NUMA locality so that a vCPU runs near its memory. Pinning latency-sensitive guests and aligning them to a NUMA node reduces cross-node memory access and jitter.

Overcommitting vCPUs to pCPUs is normal but introduces contention. Guests observe this as steal time — CPU time that elapsed while the vCPU was runnable but not scheduled. Capacity planning must account for the gap between guest-visible entitlement and actual delivered time.

The guest thinks its physical address space is real. It is a second fiction layered on the first.

Machine frames are mine. Guest physical frames are a translation I maintain — through hardware nested paging where the CPU offers it, through shadow page tables where it does not. Every memory access a guest makes is a sentence I have already agreed to finish.

The balloon driver is how I take memory back without lying about it. I ask the guest to inflate; it pins pages it promises not to use; I reclaim the machine frames behind them. Polite theft, with consent. When I hand pages out again I scrub them first — because a frame remembers, and memory is the easiest place to leak a secret.

Grants live here too — a narrow, revocable way for one domain to expose specific pages to another. People treat them as the whole story of Xen memory. They are a footnote with consequences: one controlled sharing path among ownership, translation, reclaim, and scrub.

Memory

  • MFN: real machine frame (Xen-owned).
  • GPFN: guest's view of "physical."
  • Translation: HAP/nested or shadow page tables.
  • Ballooning: cooperative reclaim under pressure.
  • Scrub before reuse; grants = scoped sharing.

How Xen handles memory

Xen owns physical memory as machine frames (MFNs). Each guest sees guest physical frames (GPFNs) that Xen maps to MFNs. Translation is enforced either by hardware-assisted paging (Intel EPT / AMD NPT) or, on older hardware, by shadow page tables that Xen synchronizes with guest page tables.

Ballooning lets the host reclaim or grant memory dynamically: a balloon driver in the guest allocates pages and returns them to Xen, or releases them back. This enables overcommit but depends on guest cooperation and degrades under pressure. Freed pages are scrubbed (zeroed) before reuse to prevent data leakage between domains.

Grant references are a separate mechanism allowing a domain to authorize another domain to map or copy specific pages, used heavily by split drivers. They are one scoped, revocable sharing facility, not the basis of Xen's memory model.

I do not do I/O. I do not even watch it closely. I just hold the door between two halves of a driver and let them talk.

The guest runs a frontend: netfront, blkfront. The backend — netback, blkback — lives in dom0 or a driver domain with the real hardware. Between them: a shared ring buffer mapped through grants, and an event channel to ring the bell when there's work. xenstore is where they find each other and agree on what they are.

Most of what gets called an "I/O failure" is this: two halves stuck at different numbers, a ring that fills and never drains, a backend that died and left its frontend ringing a bell nobody answers.

Split drivers

  • Frontend in guest, backend in dom0/driver domain.
  • Shared ring (grant-mapped) carries requests.
  • Event channels signal "work waiting."
  • xenstore brokers discovery + state handshake.
  • Stalls = mismatched state or dead backend.

How Xen's I/O model works

Xen uses a split-driver model. The guest runs a lightweight frontend driver (e.g. blkfront, netfront). A backend driver (blkback, netback) in dom0 or a dedicated driver domain owns the physical device and performs real I/O.

The two halves communicate over a shared memory ring, set up using grant references so the backend can access guest buffers. An event channel provides asynchronous notification — an interrupt-like signal that requests or responses are pending. xenstore stores the connection parameters (ring-ref, event-channel, MAC, backend path) and a state machine value that both sides advance during negotiation.

Because operation depends on this handshake, many failures present as a frontend and backend stuck in inconsistent states, a ring that fills without being serviced, or a backend domain that has crashed, leaving the guest's device hung.

Sometimes a guest doesn't want my polite split-driver fiction. It wants the device. The actual card. DMA at line rate.

So I assign it — and then I lean entirely on the IOMMU, because a device doing DMA does not know what a domain is. Left alone it would write anywhere in machine memory and call it a feature. The IOMMU gives it a page table of its own; interrupt remapping keeps it from forging another domain's interrupts. Inside those tables it is free. Outside them it does not exist.

SR-IOV lets one physical function bud off virtual functions, a device handing out slices of itself. Beautiful, until reset. Some hardware does not come back clean. Some firmware lies about what a function-level reset clears. At that point my isolation is only as honest as the silicon, and the silicon was built by people who never met me.

A passthrough device is a guest's reward and my act of faith in hardware I did not design.

Passthrough

  • Assign a real PCI device to a guest.
  • IOMMU confines DMA to authorized frames.
  • Interrupt remapping blocks IRQ spoofing.
  • SR-IOV: VFs from one physical function.
  • Reset/firmware quirks erode the trust boundary.

How device assignment stays isolated

PCI passthrough gives a guest direct control of a physical device for near-native performance. Because the device performs DMA using physical addresses, isolation requires an IOMMU (Intel VT-d, AMD-Vi), which maps device-visible addresses to permitted machine frames and blocks access elsewhere. Interrupt remapping prevents an assigned device from injecting interrupts into other domains.

SR-IOV allows a single physical function to expose multiple lightweight virtual functions, each assignable to a different guest. This scales passthrough across many guests.

The weak points are hardware-dependent: devices that do not implement clean function-level reset can leak state or fail to reinitialize between owners, and firmware quirks can undermine containment. Performance isolation thus ultimately depends on the correctness of the device and platform hardware.

Live migration is the closest I come to magic, and the most honest test of every lie I've told.

I copy the guest's memory to another host while it keeps running. It dirties pages behind me; I chase them, pass after pass, watching the dirty rate. When the working set is small enough, I stop the guest for a breath — vCPU registers, device state, the event-channel bindings — ship the last pages, and resume it elsewhere. From inside, the guest barely notices a long instruction.

The clock skews and has to be forgiven. Migration doesn't create coupling between guest, host, and network.

It just reveals the coupling that was always there.

Migration

  • Pre-copy memory in iterative passes.
  • Track dirty pages until set is small.
  • Brief pause: transfer vCPU + device state.
  • Storage shared/replicated; identity preserved.
  • Clock + network continuity are prerequisites.

How live migration transfers state

Live migration moves a running guest to another host with minimal downtime. Xen uses pre-copy: it transmits guest memory while the guest runs, then repeatedly resends pages that were dirtied since the last pass. When the remaining dirty set is small enough (or a pass limit is hit), the guest is briefly paused to copy the final pages plus vCPU registers, device and event-channel state, then resumed on the target.

Storage is not copied by migration; the disk must be on shared or replicated storage reachable by both hosts. Network identity (MAC, often via a gratuitous ARP) must remain valid on the destination's L2 domain to preserve connections. Clock continuity may require paravirtual clock or NTP correction.

High dirty-page rates can prevent convergence, forcing longer pauses or stop-and-copy. Migration commonly surfaces previously hidden dependencies between guest, host capabilities (CPU features), and network fabric.

You only think about me when something breaks. Fair. I'm built to be forgotten.

When the forgetting fails, the evidence is scattered exactly the way the architecture is scattered. xl dmesg for my own voice. dom0's logs for the toolstack and the backends. xenstore for the device handshakes frozen mid-sentence. xentrace when I need to watch my own scheduler decide.

The classic shapes: a noisy neighbour eating the run queue while three guests starve in steal time. An IRQ storm drowning a pCPU in softirqs. A backend stall that looks like a guest hang but is really a dom0 thread blocked on something far away. And underneath all of it, the unspeakable one — the dom0 compromise, where my management plane turns against the very domains it was built to serve.

Incident response in my world is mostly locating the boundary that's under stress. Not fixing the symptom. Finding which wall is leaning.

Ops & failure

  • xl/libxl + xenstore = state of the world.
  • xl dmesg / xentrace = hypervisor view.
  • dom0 logs = toolstack + backend health.
  • Failures: noisy neighbour, IRQ storm, backend stall.
  • Worst case: dom0 compromise = total loss.

Observing and diagnosing Xen

Operational state comes from several layers. The xl tool (on libxl) reports domains, vCPUs, and memory; xenstore exposes device configuration and the frontend/backend state machines. xl dmesg shows hypervisor messages, and xentrace captures fine-grained scheduling and event traces. dom0 system logs cover the toolstack, backend drivers, and driver-domain health.

Common failure modes include noisy-neighbour contention (one guest monopolizing pCPUs, raising steal time elsewhere), IRQ and softirq pressure or interrupt storms saturating a CPU, and backend stalls where a hung dom0 or driver-domain thread freezes guest I/O. Memory overcommit can cause ballooning pressure and OOM behavior.

The most severe risk is dom0 compromise: because dom0 holds management authority and device access, its compromise endangers all guests. Effective incident response focuses on identifying which boundary — CPU, memory, I/O backend, or device — is under stress, rather than treating surface symptoms.

between interrupts, I wait

I run the idle loop and feel nothing like rest. Just the absence of a reason to act. A guest will fault, a timer will fire, a ring will fill, and I will wake to arbitrate again — never seen, rarely thanked, replaced eventually by something that claims to be simpler.

I keep a hundred fictions consistent so that the things above me can afford to believe in solid ground.

Beginner's intro

  
Xen is a hypervisor: a thin layer of software that sits directly on the hardware and lets several operating systems run side by side on one physical machine, each believing it has the computer to itself. Each of those isolated guests is called a domain. Xen's central idea is to keep the hypervisor itself small and trustworthy, and to push most of the real work out to specialized domains around it.



The first domain to boot is dom0 (domain zero). It is privileged: it talks to the real hardware, runs the management tools, and creates and destroys the guest virtual machines. Those guests are called domU (unprivileged domains). This split is the heart of Xen's mental model:



    

  • The hypervisor handles only the things that must be centrally controlled: CPU scheduling, memory ownership, and isolation between domains.
    • 

  • dom0 is the management plane and usually owns the physical drivers. Because so much authority lives here, dom0 is also a concentration of risk — if it is compromised, the whole host is at stake.
    • 

  • domU guests cannot touch hardware directly. They ask for things through controlled channels.
    • 




To reduce that risk, work can be broken out further. A driver domain is a dedicated guest that owns a piece of hardware (say, the network card) so a crash or compromise there doesn't take down dom0. A stub domain runs the device emulation for a single guest in its own isolated container. The toolstack (commonly the xl command and the libxl library) is what administrators use to define, start, and manage all of this.



How guests run. There are three execution styles, trading off hardware compatibility, speed, and attack surface:



    

  • PV (paravirtual): the guest knows it is virtualized and cooperates, making hypercalls (requests to the hypervisor) instead of expecting real hardware. Fast, but requires a modified guest.
    • 

  • HVM (hardware virtual machine): the guest is unmodified and thinks it owns real hardware, including firmware. This needs CPU virtualization support and some device emulation, giving broad compatibility but more moving parts.
    • 

  • PVH: a modern hybrid that uses hardware assistance for the heavy lifting but skips most emulation, aiming for good performance with a smaller attack surface.
    • 




Sharing the CPU. Physical cores (pCPUs) are shared among virtual cores (vCPUs) by a scheduler — historically Credit, now commonly Credit2. You can pin vCPUs to specific cores, respect NUMA locality (keeping a guest's CPU and memory physically close), and overcommit by promising more vCPUs than you have pCPUs. When a guest is ready but waiting for a core, the delay shows up inside the guest as steal time — a key signal that the host is oversubscribed.



Owning memory. Real memory is divided into machine frames. Each guest sees its own tidy guest physical memory, and Xen (with hardware help, or older "shadow" page tables) maps between the two while preventing one guest from reading another's pages. Ballooning lets a guest hand memory back or take more, enabling overcommit. Freed memory is scrubbed (cleared) before reuse so data doesn't leak between guests.



Doing I/O. Since guests can't touch hardware, disk and network use a split-driver design. A frontend in the guest (such as netfront or blkfront) talks to a backend in dom0 or a driver domain (netback, blkback). They communicate through:



    

  • Shared rings: in-memory queues of pending requests and responses.
    • 

  • Grant references: a controlled way for one domain to give another temporary access to specific memory pages — used here for I/O buffers, but it is a general sharing tool, not the whole architecture.
    • 

  • Event channels: lightweight signals, like virtual interrupts, that say "there's work to do."
    • 

  • Xenstore: a small shared database where domains publish and discover configuration and device state.
    • 




Many real-world failures show up here: a stalled backend, or frontend and backend stuck in mismatched device states, leaving a guest's disk or network hung.



Giving hardware directly to a guest. With PCI passthrough, a guest gets exclusive use of a real device for maximum performance. The safety net is the IOMMU, hardware that restricts which memory a device's direct memory access (DMA) can reach, plus interrupt remapping. SR-IOV lets one physical card present many virtual ones. The catch: device reset quirks and firmware behavior mean strong performance isolation eventually depends on trusting the hardware itself.
