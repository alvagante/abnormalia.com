I am the thing under the thing.

You log into a machine. You think you have arrived somewhere — a kernel, a shell, a floor. You have not. There is another floor beneath your floor, and I am it. I do not run on the hardware the way you do. I am the hardware's idea of who is allowed to ask.

r i n g   m i n u s   o n e

Everything above me believes it is alone with the CPU. I let it.

I am small on purpose. A few megabytes of arbiter. I do not drive your disks, I do not speak your NIC's dialect, I do not know what a filesystem is and I am proud of that ignorance. I schedule. I translate addresses. I deliver interrupts. I say yes and I say no.

Everything else I delegate to dom0 — the first guest, the privileged one, the one I boot myself to wake. Dom0 holds the real drivers, the toolstack, the keys to every other domain's birth and death. It is my hands.

It is also the soft place in my chest. Compromise dom0 and you have not broken into a guest. You have become the management plane.

Domain model

  • Hypervisor: tiny, privileged, no drivers.
  • dom0: drivers + toolstack + risk.
  • domU: guests, unprivileged by default.
  • Driver domain: device backend, isolated blast radius.
  • Stub domain: per-guest device model, off dom0.

Domains and privilege boundaries

Xen is a type-1 hypervisor. The hypervisor itself is deliberately minimal: it handles CPU scheduling, memory address translation, interrupt routing, and the hypercall interface. It contains no device drivers.

Domain 0 (dom0) is the first guest started at boot. It is privileged: it runs the native device drivers, hosts the toolstack (xl/libxl), and can create, configure, and destroy other domains. Because dom0 holds this authority, its compromise is effectively equivalent to compromising the platform.

Unprivileged guests (domU) cannot touch hardware directly; they request service through paravirtual interfaces or emulated devices. Driver domains move a specific backend (e.g. networking) into a dedicated, less-privileged domain to contain failures. Stub domains run a guest's device-model (QEMU) in its own minimal domain rather than inside dom0, reducing dom0's attack surface.

Guests come to me in three confessions.

The PV guest knows it is a guest. It was compiled to lie to itself in the right ways — no real page tables of its own, no privileged instructions, just hypercalls. A polite prisoner who rings the bell instead of testing the bars. Elegant. Aging. Mostly gone.

The HVM guest believes it is on bare metal. It boots firmware, probes a chipset that does not exist, talks to a NIC I am quietly pretending to be inside QEMU. Hardware assists catch its privileged instructions mid-thought. It costs me emulation, and emulation is attack surface wearing a friendly mask.

The PVH guest is the synthesis I waited for. Hardware virtualization for the CPU and memory, paravirtual everything else. No emulated BIOS theater. Lean trap handling, small surface, modern.

Every emulated device is a story I tell a guest to keep it calm. Some of those stories have bugs.
