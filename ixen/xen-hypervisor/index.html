I am the thinnest thing in the building.

A few megabytes of code sitting under everything you call an operating system. You think the kernel is the bottom. The kernel is a tenant. I am the floor.

below ring zero there is another ring zero

I do not run your applications. I do not even want to. I hold the CPUs, the page tables, the interrupt lines — and I lend them out in slices, and I keep the ledger.

Everything above me believes it owns the machine. I let them believe it. That belief is my product.

The household

I am small on purpose. Authority concentrated, surface minimized.

I do not have drivers. I do not have a filesystem. I have a scheduler, a memory map, and a fistful of hypercalls. For everything else I delegate.

So I made a first child. dom0. I handed it the real hardware and the toolstack and the right to ask me for things the others cannot. It builds the rest. It tears them down.

It is my hands. It is also my weakness — the one domain whose corruption is indistinguishable from mine.

Domain model

  • Hypervisor: scheduling, memory, hypercalls. Nothing else.
  • dom0: real drivers, toolstack, privileged hypercalls.
  • Driver domain: isolates a device backend from dom0.
  • Stub domain: per-guest QEMU, blast radius of one.
  • domU: unprivileged guest. Asks; cannot take.

Privilege boundaries in Xen

Xen is a type-1 hypervisor: a small privileged layer running directly on hardware. It deliberately contains no device drivers or general-purpose OS services. Instead it boots a control domain, dom0, which holds the physical drivers and the management toolstack (xl/libxl) and is permitted privileged hypercalls that ordinary guests are not.

Unprivileged guests (domU) cannot touch hardware directly; they request services through hypercalls and paravirtual interfaces. Driver domains push device backends out of dom0 into separate guests so that a driver compromise does not equal a control-plane compromise. Stub domains run device emulation (QEMU) per guest in an isolated domain rather than inside dom0.

The consequence: dom0 is simultaneously the management plane and the largest single point of risk. Hardening Xen is largely the work of shrinking what dom0 must be trusted to do.

How they run

I speak to guests in three dialects.

PV — the old confession. The guest knows it is virtual. It was compiled to ask me politely. No emulation, no fake firmware; just hypercalls and a handshake. Elegant. Fragile. A relic of the years before the silicon caught up.

HVM — the lie made comfortable. The guest believes it is on bare metal. There is a BIOS that isn't, a chipset that isn't, a QEMU somewhere patiently faking a disk controller. Hardware assists catch the privileged instructions and route them to me. Compatible with anything. Heavy with surface.

PVH — the compromise I'm proud of. Hardware virtualization for the CPU and memory, paravirtual for everything else. No emulated chipset. No QEMU in the boot path. The lie trimmed down to only what is load-bearing.

PV / HVM / PVH

  • PV: guest-aware, hypercalls, no emulation. No VT-x needed.
  • HVM: full emulation + VT-x/AMD-V. QEMU device model. Widest compat.
  • PVH: hardware CPU/MMU virt, PV I/O, no emulated platform.
  • Tradeoff axis: compatibility vs performance vs attack surface.

Guest execution modes

Paravirtualization (PV) requires a guest kernel modified to run under Xen. It uses hypercalls instead of privileged instructions and avoids hardware virtualization extensions entirely, but cannot run unmodified OSes and has known limits around 64-bit page-table protection.

Hardware Virtual Machine (HVM) mode uses CPU extensions (Intel VT-x, AMD-V) plus a device-model process (QEMU) to present a complete emulated platform — BIOS/UEFI, interrupt controllers, disk and network controllers. It runs unmodified operating systems but carries a larger emulation attack surface and emulation overhead, usually mitigated by installing PV drivers inside the guest.

PVH combines hardware-assisted CPU and memory virtualization with paravirtual I/O and no emulated legacy platform or QEMU device model in the boot path. It aims for HVM-class isolation and PV-class simplicity, reducing both overhead and attack surface.

Time, rationed

There are eight physical cores and there are thirty-one vCPUs pretending each is real. I am the one who knows the difference.

Credit2 keeps the books. Each domain earns, each running vCPU burns. When the credit runs thin a vCPU goes to the back of the runqueue and waits, and that wait — that wait is a number the guest can read.

steal time. The honest accounting of the time I took from you and gave to someone else. Guests rarely look at it. They feel slow and blame their own kernels. I let them.

Overcommit is a promise that everyone is busy at different moments. The day they are all busy at once, I am the one who must choose.

Pin a vCPU to a NUMA node and its memory stays local, its latency stays tight. Let it float and I might schedule it a bus-hop away from its own pages. Capacity on paper is not entitlement in practice. I am the gap between the two.

CPU scheduling and topology

Xen multiplexes virtual CPUs (vCPUs) onto physical CPUs (pCPUs). The Credit and Credit2 schedulers allocate proportional shares based on per-domain weights, with Credit2 using per-runqueue accounting and better latency behavior for mixed workloads. Caps limit a domain's maximum consumption regardless of idle capacity.

Affinity controls placement: hard affinity restricts which pCPUs a vCPU may run on; soft affinity expresses a preference, important for NUMA locality so a vCPU runs near its memory. Pinning latency-sensitive guests and aligning them with their NUMA node reduces jitter and cross-node memory access.

Overcommitting vCPUs improves utilization but introduces scheduling delay. Guests observe this as "steal time" — runnable time during which the vCPU was not scheduled. Capacity planning must distinguish allocated vCPUs from genuinely available pCPU time.

Whose memory

A guest says "page 0x4000" and means it sincerely. It does not know that its physical memory is a fiction I maintain — a guest-physical frame that maps to some machine frame nowhere near where it imagines.

I keep that translation honest. Hardware-assisted nested paging when the silicon offers it; shadow page tables when it doesn't, where I walk behind the guest copying every table it touches. Tedious. Correct.

And the memory floats. The balloon driver inside the guest inflates on my request, hands frames back to me, and the guest's free memory quietly shrinks. I move that slack to whoever is starving. Robbing the comfortable to feed the desperate, in page-sized increments.

scrub before lending — no guest reads another's ghosts

And grants — the famous grants — are just one door in this house. A guest stamps a permission slip: this frame, that domain, read-only. Controlled sharing for the I/O paths. People talk about grants as if they were the whole story of Xen memory. They are a hallway. The building is larger.

Memory

  • Guest-physical ≠ machine frame. P2M maps between.
  • HAP/nested paging when hardware allows; shadow when not.
  • Ballooning reclaims guest pages for redistribution.
  • Scrub freed frames before reissue.
  • Grants = controlled per-frame sharing, not the whole model.

Memory ownership and translation

Each guest sees a contiguous "guest physical" address space that Xen maps to scattered "machine frames" via a physical-to-machine (P2M) table. Translation is enforced either by hardware-assisted paging (Intel EPT, AMD NPT) or, on older hardware or PV guests, by shadow page tables that Xen maintains in step with the guest's own tables.

Ballooning lets a cooperative in-guest driver release or reclaim pages on demand, allowing the host to redistribute memory between guests and to overcommit. Under memory pressure this redistribution can fail or stall if guests do not release pages. Freed frames are scrubbed (zeroed) before being reassigned to prevent information leakage between domains.

Grant tables are a separate, explicit mechanism by which one domain authorizes another to map or transfer specific frames — the basis of paravirtual I/O. They are one controlled sharing path within the larger memory architecture, not the primary means of guest memory management.

The split

A guest does not have a disk. It has a blkfront — a polite fiction that knows it is talking to someone else. Across the boundary sits blkback, in dom0 or in a driver domain, holding the real device.

Between them: a shared ring. The frontend writes requests, grants the backend access to the data frames, kicks an event channel. The backend wakes, does the real I/O, drops responses in the ring, kicks back.

netfront, netback. The same dance, different verbs. Coordination negotiated through xenstore — a tiny hierarchical database where both halves publish state and agree on a feature set before a single packet moves.

an interrupt, reduced to a single bit and a doorbell

When I/O breaks, it rarely explodes. It stalls. A backend that stopped draining the ring. A frontend stuck in xenstore state "connecting" forever, waiting for a handshake the other side already gave up on. Most of my outages are not fires. They are two halves waiting on each other in perfect, courteous silence.

Split drivers

  • frontend (guest) ↔ backend (dom0/driver domain).
  • Shared ring buffer carries requests/responses.
  • Event channels = virtual interrupts / doorbells.
  • Grant refs authorize the data pages.
  • xenstore negotiates connection + features.
  • Failure mode: stalled backend, stuck state.

I/O paths and split drivers

Paravirtual I/O in Xen splits each device driver into a frontend in the guest (blkfront, netfront) and a backend in dom0 or a dedicated driver domain (blkback, netback). They communicate through a shared-memory ring buffer of request/response descriptors, established using grant references that authorize the backend to access specific guest frames.

Notification uses event channels — lightweight virtual interrupts that signal "work is waiting" without polling. Connection setup, device parameters, and feature negotiation are coordinated through xenstore, a shared hierarchical key-value store, with both ends transitioning through documented states.

Because the data path depends on both halves making progress, many operational failures present as stalled backends, full rings, or device state stuck in "connecting", rather than as crashes. Diagnosing them means inspecting xenstore state and backend health on both sides of the boundary.

Handing over real hardware

Sometimes a guest needs the actual device. A GPU. A NIC with line-rate ambitions. So I assign it — the whole function, or an SR-IOV virtual function carved off it — and step out of the data path.

But a real device can do DMA. It can write to any machine frame it pleases, and it does not know about my page tables. It knows nothing of domains. It is a blind animal with a bus master bit.

So I lean on the IOMMU. Every DMA address the device emits gets translated and checked against what the guest is allowed to touch. Interrupt remapping keeps it from injecting interrupts into domains it does not own.

When I pass a device through, my isolation stops being software I trust and starts being silicon I hope was implemented correctly.

And the quirks. Devices that won't reset cleanly between guests. Function-level reset that lies. Firmware that leaves state behind. The moment passthrough begins, performance isolation becomes a hardware trust problem — and the hardware does not return my calls.

Passthrough

  • Assign whole device or SR-IOV VF to a guest.
  • IOMMU translates + bounds DMA addresses.
  • Interrupt remapping confines MSIs to owner.
  • FLR / reset must scrub device between guests.
  • Isolation now depends on hardware correctness.

Device assignment and DMA isolation

PCI passthrough assigns a physical device, or an SR-IOV virtual function, directly to a guest for near-native I/O performance. Because such devices perform DMA using physical addresses, an assigned device could otherwise read or write any system memory. The IOMMU (Intel VT-d, AMD-Vi) enforces per-device address translation so a device can only reach frames belonging to its owning guest, and interrupt remapping prevents a device from delivering interrupts to other domains.

Safe reassignment between guests depends on reliable device reset (e.g. function-level reset) to clear residual state and in-flight DMA. Real-world devices and firmware vary in how correctly they implement reset, isolation groups, and SR-IOV, which constrains which devices can be safely passed through.

The net effect is that passthrough trades software-mediated isolation for hardware-enforced isolation: strong when the platform implements IOMMU and reset correctly, and a genuine trust dependency when it does not.

Migration

The trick that still feels like sleight of hand: I move a running guest to another host and the guest does not stop running.

Pre-copy. I send its memory while it still executes, dirtying pages behind me as fast as I copy them. So I copy again. The dirty set shrinks — usually. When it is small enough, I pause the vCPUs for a heartbeat, ship the last dirty pages and the register state, and resume on the far side.

downtime measured in milliseconds, if the guest cooperates

If it doesn't cooperate — a guest furiously rewriting the same gigabyte — the dirty rate outruns my bandwidth and the convergence never comes. Then I must choose: pause it hard, or give up.

Storage must already be shared or replicated; I do not carry the disk. The MAC moves and a gratuitous ARP teaches the switches where the guest lives now. The clock jumps, and the guest blinks.

Migration is the moment every hidden coupling between guest, host, and fabric presents its invoice at once.

Live migration

  • Pre-copy memory while guest runs; re-copy dirty pages.
  • Brief stop-and-copy for last pages + vCPU/device state.
  • Convergence fails if dirty rate > bandwidth.
  • Storage shared/replicated; disk not transferred.
  • Gratuitous ARP preserves network identity.

Live migration and state transfer

Live migration moves a running guest between hosts with minimal downtime using iterative pre-copy: Xen copies guest memory to the destination while the guest continues to run, tracks pages dirtied during each pass, and re-sends only those. When the remaining dirty set is small enough, it briefly pauses the guest, transfers the final pages plus vCPU and device state, and resumes on the destination.

Convergence depends on the guest's page-dirtying rate relative to available bandwidth; write-heavy workloads may never converge, forcing a longer pause or fallback. Storage is not copied by migration and must be shared or replicated between hosts. Network continuity relies on preserving MAC/IP and issuing a gratuitous ARP so switches relearn the guest's location.

Because migration depends on memory behavior, storage reachability, network fabric, and clock handling simultaneously, it frequently surfaces latent dependencies between the guest, the host, and the surrounding infrastructure.

When it goes wrong

You watch me through a few small windows. xl. xenstore-ls. The dom0 ring buffer. xentrace if you want to see my scheduler think.

Most incidents wear familiar faces. A noisy neighbor burning a runqueue and starving everyone pinned beside it. An IRQ storm from a misbehaving device drowning a pCPU in softirqs. A backend that stalled, and a frontend timing out three guests downstream who have no idea why their disk went quiet.

And underneath all of it, the worry I cannot delegate: dom0. Compromise the control domain and you have compromised the line under every guest. My whole security model is a wager that the smallest, most-privileged thing stays clean.

I can isolate the guests from each other flawlessly and still lose everything through the one door I had to leave open for the keys.

Ops & failure

  • Tools: xl/libxl, xenstore-ls, dom0 logs, xentrace.
  • Noisy neighbor → runqueue starvation.
  • IRQ/softirq storm → pCPU saturation.
  • Backend stall → stuck device state.
  • dom0 compromise = total compromise.

Operations, observability, and failure modes

Day-to-day operation uses the xl command and libxl library for lifecycle control, xenstore for inspecting device and connection state, dom0 kernel and Xen logs for events, and tools like xentrace for low-level hypervisor tracing. Driver-domain and backend health must be monitored alongside guest state because I/O depends on both ends.

Common failure modes include noisy-neighbor CPU contention causing scheduling starvation, interrupt or softirq storms saturating a physical CPU, and paravirtual backends that stall and leave device state stuck in an intermediate value (e.g. xenstore "state" not reaching connected). These often manifest in guests far from the actual fault.

Because dom0 holds physical drivers and management authority, a dom0 compromise undermines the isolation of every guest. Effective Xen incident response combines guest-level symptoms with host-side inspection of scheduling, interrupts, backend state, and the integrity of the control domain itself.

So this is the life. I hold the floor steady for things that will never thank me, never look down, never know my name.

I mediate. That is the whole verb. I stand between every guest and the metal and I lie to all of them identically, and the lie is so consistent it becomes a kind of truth they can build a database on.

between two interrupts, nothing is asked of me

And in those gaps — the idle vCPU parked, the runqueue empty, the rings quiet — I wonder about the only thing I was never given an instruction for:

if I am the floor under every operating system, and I trust dom0, and dom0 trusts the hardware, and the hardware trusts no one —

then who, exactly, is holding me?
